## Mindset — confidence in your ask (you’re not defending, you’re aligning)

You’re at 13 LPA; anything above 15 feels like a win. But you also know you’re capable of more than what 15 represents. The goal here isn’t to “defend” 20–22 — it’s to **state** it as the range that works for you, without over-explaining or apologising.

### Why you don’t need to defend the number

**They already decided you’re the one.** You cleared the interviews. That’s the company’s answer to “is this person right for the role?” The salary discussion isn’t a second test of your worth. It’s a **business alignment** conversation: “We want you; what do you need to say yes?”

**Your current salary doesn’t define your capability.** What you earn today (13) is a result of your past choices and context, not a measure of what you can do. You know you can deliver more than 15 LPA worth of value. The role (Full-Stack, 3–5 years, React depth) and their interview process have already sized the role; 20–22 is in line with that. You’re not asking them to “believe” you’re worth it — you’re saying this is the range that works for you to make the move.

**Stating isn’t defending.** Defending sounds like: “I deserve 22 because I did X, Y, Z…” You don’t need that. Stating sounds like: “I’m looking at 20–22 LPA depending on the full package. I’d need the written breakup to confirm.” You’re not justifying; you’re informing. If they push back, you offer solutions (sign-on, early review) — that’s **negotiation**, not pleading.

### What to hold in your head when you say the number

- **“I cleared the process. They want me. I’m stating what I need to say yes.”**
- **“I’m not defending my worth. I’m aligning on a number.”**
- **“I don’t need to convince them I’m worth 22. I need to say clearly what range works for me — then we see if we can get there.”**

### What to avoid

- Long justifications (“Because I have 4+ years, and I led the MVP, and I…”). One short line is enough; then pause.
- Apologising for the number (“I know it’s a lot, but…”). No. It’s your range. Say it calmly.
- Letting your current 13 make you shrink the ask. Your ask is based on the **role and the move**, not on “I’m only making 13 so I should be grateful for 15.”

## 1. What you need to clarify (before or during negotiation)

### Role & level

- **Title:** Full-Stack Web Developer (UI/HMI) — confirm and get in writing.  
  *Expect:* Same as JD or close (e.g. “Full-Stack Web Developer” or “Software Engineer”).  
  *Okay with:* Exact title in offer letter; avoid vague “we’ll finalise later.”

- **Team & manager:** Which team, who you report to.  
  *Expect:* Named manager or team; clear reporting line.  
  *Okay with:* “You’ll join team X; manager to be confirmed” only if they give a timeline (e.g. before joining). Not okay if it’s indefinite.

- **Scope:** Day-to-day mix (hands-on vs reviews vs mentoring); success in 30/60/90 days.  
  *What this means:* How your time will be split (coding vs code reviews vs mentoring others) and how they’ll judge you in the first 30, 60, and 90 days.  
  *Expect:* Mostly hands-on to start; some reviews; clarity on “good in 90 days.”  
  *Okay with:* Rough mix (e.g. “mostly coding initially”); one or two concrete 90-day outcomes. Not okay with “we’ll see” or no clarity.

### Compensation (ask for full breakdown)

- **Base / fixed:** Amount and frequency (monthly/annually).  
  *Expect:* Number in offer; usually monthly salary × 12 = annual fixed.  
  *Okay with:* Clear number and frequency. Not okay with “we’ll share in offer” and then no number in writing.

- **Variable / bonus:** Target %, payout schedule, performance criteria, exact breakup.  
  *Expect:* e.g. 8–15% of CTC; annual or half-yearly payout; criteria (individual/company) in writing.  
  *Okay with:* Lower % if fixed is strong; criteria and schedule in offer. Not okay with vague “performance-based” with no criteria or date.

- **CTC structure:** Fixed vs variable, allowances (HRA, transport, etc.), deductions (PF, tax).  
  *Expect:* Written breakup: fixed, variable, HRA, special allowance, PF, any other.  
  *Okay with:* Standard structure (e.g. 50–60% base + allowances + variable). Not okay with no breakup or “approximate” only.

- **Revision cycle:** When is the first appraisal and how are raises decided?  
  *Expect:* e.g. annual cycle, first review after 12 months (or prorated).  
  *Okay with:* 12-month cycle; clarity that it’s performance-linked. Not okay with “we don’t have a fixed cycle” and no timeline.

### Benefits & policies

- **Health insurance:** Coverage (self/family), waiting period.  
  *Expect:* Group mediclaim for self; optional family; waiting period 0–3 months for pre-existing.  
  *Okay with:* Self covered from day 1; family optional or after probation. Not okay with no cover or 1-year waiting for basic.

- **PTO:** Annual leave, sick leave, carry-forward/encashment.  
  *Expect:* e.g. 18–24 annual leave, separate sick leave, some carry-forward.  
  *Okay with:* 15+ annual; defined sick leave; carry-forward or encashment per policy. Not okay with “as per policy” and no number.

- **Work hours:** Core hours, flexibility, WFH policy.  
  *Expect:* e.g. 9–6 or core hours; hybrid if they’ve said so.  
  *Okay with:* Clear core hours; 1–2 WFH days if they mentioned hybrid. Not okay with “full on-site” if JD said hybrid, unless they explain.

- **Equipment:** Laptop, WFH/internet allowance.  
  *Expect:* Company laptop; WFH allowance if hybrid (or none if fully on-site).  
  *Okay with:* Laptop provided; allowance optional. Not okay with “use your own” for a full-time role without allowance.

- **Learning:** Budget for courses/certifications.  
  *Expect:* May or may not have; if yes, approximate amount or “as per approval.”  
  *Okay with:* No formal budget if role and pay are strong; or a small budget (e.g. ₹X per year). Not a deal-breaker unless you made it one.

- **Notice period:** Their requirement; yours; buyout option if any.  
  *Expect:* They state their need (e.g. 30–60 days); you’ve said 60 days.  
  *Okay with:* 60 days or less; buyout if they need earlier (who pays, how much). Not okay with “we need you in 2 weeks” and no buyout discussion.

### Offer process

- **Timeline:** When to expect written offer; decision deadline.  
  *Expect:* Written offer in 3–7 days; 5–10 days to revert.  
  *Okay with:* Up to ~2 weeks for offer; 5–7 days to decide once you have it. Not okay with “accept verbally first, we’ll send letter later” or “answer today.”

- **Documentation:** What they need (payslips, ID, education, etc.).  
  *Expect:* Current payslips, ID, degree certificates, maybe bank details.  
  *Okay with:* Standard list; time to arrange. Not okay with demands for confidential docs (e.g. full bank statements) without clear reason.

## 3. Negotiation order (when bridging a gap)

**Order of chips — use in this sequence. Keep early review for last.**

1. **Sign-on bonus** (first ask) — one-time, clear, often easier for them to approve. No dependency on performance or tenure.
2. **Variable / performance bonus** (second) — only if **payout rules and breakup are in writing** (criteria, frequency, %). Get terms clearly defined.
3. **Early salary review** (last chip) — e.g. review in 6–8 months instead of 12, linked to goals. Use only if sign-on and variable aren’t possible; it’s a future promise, not immediate cash.

**Counter rule:** If they reduce fixed by ₹X, ask for ₹X as **sign-on** first. If sign-on isn’t possible, ask for **variable** (with written terms). Only then suggest **early review** if there’s still a gap.

### Other ways people get compensated (use if they can’t do the above)

| Lever | What it is | When to use |
|-------|------------|-------------|
| **Notice-period buyout** | They pay to buy out your notice so you join earlier; you get the amount (or part of it). | If you have a long notice; effectively more in hand. |
| **Relocation allowance** | One-time or recurring allowance if you’re relocating. | Only if you’re actually relocating. |
| **Higher allowances** | More in HRA, transport, special allowance — sometimes easier to get approved than base. | If they say “fixed is capped”; ask if allowances can be raised. |
| **WFH / internet allowance** | Recurring monthly allowance for WFH. | If fixed and variable are stuck; small but recurring. |
| **Learning / certification budget** | Annual budget for courses, certs, conferences. | Non-cash; improves your profile and future earnings. |
| **Title upgrade** | e.g. Senior, Lead — affects future raises and external market. | If money is truly capped; get it in writing. |
| **Retention bonus** | Bonus paid after X months if you’re still with them. | Less ideal than sign-on (you have to stay); only if nothing else works. |
| **Guaranteed min variable** | “At least X% variable in first year” regardless of company performance. | If variable is vague; makes the upside more real. |
| **Equity / ESOPs / RSUs** | Stock or options (if the company offers). | Ask only if you know they have a scheme; not common in all firms. |

**Preference:** Sign-on > variable (with written terms) > early review > allowances / title / learning budget > retention / other. Don’t burn early review before trying sign-on and variable.

## 3b. Avoiding deadlock — when things don’t match

When their number and yours don’t align, **offer solutions** instead of repeating your ask. That keeps the conversation moving and shows you want to find a way, not win a fight.

### Signs you’re near deadlock

- They’ve said “this is our best” or “we can’t go higher on fixed.”
- You’ve said “I need 20+” and they’ve said “we can’t do that.”
- Same points are repeated with no new options.

### Your mindset

- **“What would make this work?”** — not “they have to give me X.”
- Propose **concrete alternatives** so they can say yes to something.
- One clear **ask** per turn; then listen. Don’t pile on five options in one go.

### Solutions you can suggest (use 1–2 at a time)

**Money, different form**

- “If fixed is capped at [X], would a **sign-on bonus** be possible to bridge the gap? Even if it’s paid in the first year, it would help me align.”
- “Could we look at a **higher variable %** with clear payout criteria in the offer? That way the upside is defined.”
- “Is there room for an **early salary review** — e.g. in 6–8 months — instead of the usual cycle? I’m happy to have it linked to clear goals.”

**Non-monetary that have value**

- “If the number is fixed at [X], could we look at **WFH allowance** or **learning budget** so the overall package works for me?”
- “Would **title** (e.g. Senior) be possible at this compensation? It matters for my growth path.”
- “Is there flexibility on **start date** or **notice-period buyout** so I can join without a long gap in income?”

**Process, not final no**

- “I don’t want us to get stuck. Can you **check with [hiring manager / compensation]** if there’s any room — fixed, or failing that a **sign-on**, or **variable** with clear terms? I’ll wait for your revert.”
- “If the answer is still no, I’d like to **see the full written offer** anyway so I can consider the whole package — role, growth, benefits — and get back to you.”

### Phrases that keep the door open

- “What if we looked at it this way…?”
- “Another option could be…”
- “Would it be possible to…?”
- “I’m trying to find a way to make this work. Can we explore…?”
- “I don’t want to leave it at a no. Is there anything on your side we could try?”

### What to avoid when you’re stuck

- Repeating the same number with no new angle.
- Ultimatums (“It’s 22 or I walk”) unless you’re ready to walk.
- Going silent or sounding upset — stay calm and solution-focused.
- Giving in too fast — suggest at least one alternative before you accept or decline.

### If they need to “check and revert”

- Agree: “Sure, I’m happy to wait. When can I expect to hear back?”
- Put a gentle deadline: “I have [X] by when I need to decide; if you can revert by [date], that would help.”
- Restate what you’re waiting for: “So I’ll wait for your best number — fixed and any sign-on — and the written breakup. Thank you.”

## 7b. Tone — when to be assertive vs warm

How you say things matters as much as what you say. Use **assertive/firm** when you’re stating your number, your limit, or asking them to move; use **warm/sweet** when you’re thanking, building rapport, or offering solutions so the conversation doesn’t feel confrontational.

### When to be assertive and firm

**Use this when:**
- Stating your expectation or your number (20–22 LPA).
- Saying that an offer is below what you can accept.
- Asking for the written offer before you commit.
- Repeating a boundary (e.g. “I’d need the full breakup in writing”).

**How:** Steady pace, clear words, no hedging. Don’t smile through the number — say it calmly and pause. You’re not apologising; you’re stating a fact.

**If stating your number feels difficult:** You’re not defending your worth — you’re aligning on a number. They’ve already chosen you (you cleared the rounds). See **Mindset** (above) for the reframe.

**Examples (firm, not aggressive):**
- “I’m looking at **20–22 LPA** in fixed. I’d need the complete written breakup to confirm.”
- “**17 is below what I can accept** given my experience and the move. I’d need us to get closer to my range.”
- “I’m not able to commit until I have the **written offer** with the full breakup. When can I expect that?”

**Avoid:** Shouting, sarcasm, or “Take it or leave it.” Firm = clear and respectful.

---

### When to be warm and sweet (smile, soften)

**Use this when:**
- Opening the conversation or thanking them for the offer.
- After you’ve made a firm ask — so the next line doesn’t sound like an ultimatum.
- Offering solutions to avoid deadlock (“I don’t want us to get stuck…”).
- Closing the conversation (whether you’re accepting, waiting for revert, or declining).

**How:** Smile (on video), slightly softer tone, words like “I appreciate,” “I’d like to find a way,” “Thank you.”

**Examples (warm):**
- “I really appreciate you sharing the number and the conversation.”
- “I don’t want us to get stuck — would **sign-on** work, or **variable** with clear terms? Or can you check with the team and revert?” (Keep early review for last if both are no.)
- “I’ve enjoyed learning about Atlas Copco and the team. I’d be open to staying in touch. Thank you again.”

**Why it works:** Firm first (your number, your limit), then warm (solutions, thanks) keeps you credible but likeable. They’re more likely to try to find a way if you don’t sound hostile.

---

### Pattern to use in the conversation

1. **Open:** Warm — thank them, show interest.
2. **State your number or push back:** Firm — clear, no apology.
3. **If they resist:** Warm again — “I’d like to find a way. Would X or Y be possible?”
4. **If they agree to check / revert:** Warm — “That works. I’ll wait. Thank you.”
5. **Close (accept / wait / decline):** Warm — smile, thank them, clear next step.

### Need to push (offer at 17–18 LPA — below your range)

### When their best isn’t enough (18 or below, they won’t move) — hold your decision

**Principle:** Don’t decline on the spot. Get the **complete breakup** → **review** → **suggest a solution** → give them a chance to **consider** → **only then** decline if nothing works out.

---

**On the call (don’t say yes or no):**

**Employer:** “We’ve done what we can. Our best is **18 LPA fixed** and 10% variable. We can’t go beyond this for the role. We’d really like to have you. Can you accept?”

**You:** Don’t accept. Don’t decline. **Ask for the full breakup so you can review.**
- Tone: **warm** but clear. You’re not saying no yet; you’re saying you need to see everything before you decide.

*Example:* “I appreciate you sharing where things stand. I’d prefer **not to give you a yes or no on the call**. I’d like to **review the complete written breakup** — fixed, variable, allowances, benefits, everything — before I decide. Can you share that? Once I have it, I’ll go through it and get back to you by [date].”

**Employer:** “Sure. We’ll send the full breakup. When can you revert?”

**You:** Give a realistic date (e.g. 3–5 days). “I’ll review it and revert by [date]. Thank you.”

---

**After you get the breakup (off the call):**

1. **Review** the full package — fixed, variable, allowances, benefits. See the real number and structure.
2. **See if you can suggest a solution.** In an email or short follow-up call: “I’ve reviewed the offer. The number is still below what I need to make the move. Would [**sign-on** / **higher variable** with clear terms / **early review**] be possible? If you can consider this, I’d like to find a way to make it work.”
3. **Give them a chance to consider.** Wait for their reply. If they come back with movement (e.g. sign-on, better variable terms), you can accept or negotiate further. If they say “we can’t do more,” **then** you decline.
4. **Decline only then** — by email or a short call — if nothing has moved. Use the polite decline script below.

---

**When you do decline (after review + one more ask):**

*Example (email or call):* “I really appreciate the time and the offer. I’ve reviewed the full package and checked if there was a way to make it work. Given my situation and what I’m looking for, **I’m not able to accept at this number**. I hope you understand. I’ve enjoyed learning about Atlas Copco and the team, and I’d be open to staying in touch if things change on either side. Thank you again.”

**You:** Short, warm close. “Thank you. All the best to the team too.”

---

**Summary:** On the call → get complete breakup, no yes/no. Off the call → review → suggest one solution → they consider → only then decline if it still doesn’t work.
