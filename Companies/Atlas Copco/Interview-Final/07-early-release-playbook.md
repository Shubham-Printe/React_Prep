# Early release playbook — Secure 30–45 days instead of 60

**Goal:** Get your current employer to agree to release you in 30 or 45 days (instead of 60) so you can join Atlas Copco without a long gap. This playbook assumes you **only** request early release (no “I’ll leave without serving” — that has legal/reputation risk).

---

## Principle: Make it easy for them to say yes

They care about: (1) work not dropping, (2) no bad blood, (3) policy not being undermined in a way that sets a bad precedent. Your job is to **remove their reasons to say no**: handover plan, clear owner, overlap, and (if needed) buyout so the company doesn’t “lose” by letting you go early.

---

## Phase 0: Before you say a word to your current employer

Do this **only after you have the Atlas Copco offer in hand** (signed or at least final and acceptable). If you ask for early release before that and the offer falls through, you’ve already signalled exit.

### 0.1 Check your contract

- [ ] Notice period: confirm it’s 60 days (or whatever is in writing).
- [ ] Any mention of **buyout** (pay to reduce notice)?
- [ ] **Garden leave** (they tell you to stay home but pay you for the notice)?
- [ ] Any **non-compete** or cooling-off that could affect joining Atlas Copco?

You don’t need to announce anything; just re-read the offer letter / appointment letter you have.

### 0.2 Prepare your handover document (before the conversation)

This is your **biggest lever**. Managers are far more likely to agree when they see you’re serious about leaving the team in good shape.

**Create a short document (one page is enough) with:**

| Section | What to put |
|--------|-------------|
| **Current ownership** | Features, modules, repos, or clients you own or lead. |
| **Pending / in-flight** | Key tasks, deadlines, and who you suggest takes them over. |
| **Handover plan** | “I will: (1) Document X, Y; (2) Do KT with [person] over [number] sessions; (3) Be available for questions until my last day.” |
| **Suggested timeline** | e.g. “30 days from today: Week 1–2 KT and docs; Week 3–4 overlap with new owner; LWD [date].” |

You don’t have to share the full doc in the first sentence, but **have it ready**. When they say “how will we manage?”, you say “I’ve put together a handover plan — I can share it right after this call.”

### 0.3 Decide your “ask” and fallback

- **Preferred:** 30 days from the day you resign (or from a specific date you’ll name).
- **Fallback:** 45 days if they push back on 30.
- **Last resort:** 60 days (full notice) — no early release, but you’re still leaving on good terms. You can still ask Atlas Copco if they’re okay with that joining date.

### 0.4 (Optional) Check buyout

If your contract or employee handbook mentions notice-period buyout, note the amount (e.g. 1 month salary, 2 months). If not, you can ask HR during or after the conversation: “Is there a buyout option for notice period, and what would be the amount?” Have this in mind so you can mention it to your manager if they say “policy is 60 days.”

---

## Phase 1: The conversation with your manager

**Who:** Your direct manager.  
**When:** After you have the Atlas Copco offer. Ideally within 1–2 days of accepting, so they hear it from you first.  
**Where:** One-on-one, preferably video or in person. Not in a busy corridor or over casual chat.

### 1.1 Opening (short and clear)

You’re not apologising; you’re informing and requesting.

**Script:**

“I need to share something with you. I’ve accepted an opportunity elsewhere and I’ll be resigning. My contractual notice is 60 days, but the new role needs me to join sooner. I’d like to request an early release — I’m thinking 30 days from [today / a specific date]. I’ve prepared a handover plan so the team isn’t left in a lurch — I can share it with you and we can align on ownership and KT. I’d really appreciate your support on this.”

Then **pause**. Let them respond.

### 1.2 If they ask questions

| They say | You say |
|----------|---------|
| “Why are you leaving?” | Keep it neutral: “It’s a role and domain I want to grow in. I’ve enjoyed working here and I want to leave on a good note.” |
| “Can you stay longer?” | “I’ve committed to a joining date. I can do up to 45 days if that helps, but 30 would be ideal.” |
| “How will we manage the work?” | “I’ve put together a handover plan — current ownership, pending items, suggested owners, and a KT schedule. I’ll share it right after this and we can adjust.” |
| “Policy is 60 days.” | “I understand. I’m requesting an exception given the handover I’m offering. If there’s no flexibility, is there a buyout option so I can be released earlier?” |
| “Who will take over?” | Name 1–2 people you’ve thought of (from your handover doc). “I’d suggest [name] for [area]. I’m happy to do proper KT with them.” |

### 1.3 If they say yes (30 or 45 days)

- Thank them.
- Confirm the **last working day (LWD)** clearly. “So my last working day will be [date]. I’ll send you the handover doc by [date] and we can align on KT.”
- Send the handover document within 24–48 hours. Stick to the plan. This builds trust and makes it easier for them to justify the exception to HR if needed.
- Update Atlas Copco HR with the confirmed joining date.

### 1.4 If they say “I need to check with HR” or “I’ll get back to you”

- “Sure. I’ll send you the handover plan in the meantime so you have it when you discuss. Please let me know if HR needs any input from me.”
- Send the handover doc. Follow up once after 2–3 days if you haven’t heard back: “Just checking if there’s any update on the early release. I’m happy to jump on a call if needed.”

### 1.5 If they say no (we can’t do less than 60 days)

- Stay calm. “I understand. I’ll serve the full 60 days then. I’ll still send the handover plan and do a proper KT so the transition is smooth.”
- Ask: “Is there a buyout option in policy? If I could pay or if the new employer could reimburse, would that allow an earlier release?” (Some companies allow buyout even when “exceptions” are not allowed.)
- If no buyout and no exception: your LWD is 60 days from the day you resign. Inform Atlas Copco of the joining date (60 days from resignation) and ask if they’re okay with it. Many companies are, especially for a strong hire.

---

## Phase 2: If HR is involved

Sometimes the manager says “this is an HR decision” or HR reaches out after your manager tells them.

### 2.1 With HR

- Stay professional. You’re not arguing; you’re requesting.
- Emphasise: “I’ve committed to a detailed handover and KT. My manager has seen the plan. I’m not leaving work hanging.”
- Ask explicitly: “Is there a notice-period buyout option? I’m happy to discuss terms so the company isn’t at a loss.”
- If they say no to early release and no to buyout: accept it, confirm your LWD, and still deliver the handover. Burning the bridge helps no one.

### 2.2 Get it in writing

Once **any** early release is agreed (30, 45, or buyout), ask for a short confirmation in email. For example: “As discussed, my last working day will be [date]. Please confirm when you get a chance.” That protects you and gives Atlas Copco something to rely on.

---

## Phase 3: What to tell Atlas Copco

| Situation | What to tell Atlas Copco HR |
|-----------|-----------------------------|
| Early release agreed (e.g. 30 days) | “I’ve secured an early release. My last working day is [date]; I can join from [date]. Please issue the offer with this joining date.” |
| Still in discussion | “I’m in discussion with my current employer for an early release. My contractual notice is 60 days; I’m requesting 30. I’ll confirm the exact joining date by [date].” |
| Only 60 days possible | “I’ll need to serve the full 60 days. So my joining date would be [date]. Please let me know if that works.” |
| Buyout possible | “They’ve agreed to early release with a buyout of [amount]. Does Atlas Copco support notice-period buyout or reimbursement?” |

## Quick checklist (in order)

- [ ] **Offer in hand** — Don’t request early release before you have the Atlas Copco offer.
- [ ] **Contract check** — Notice period, buyout, garden leave (no need to ask HR yet).
- [ ] **Handover doc ready** — Ownership, pending work, suggested owners, KT plan, timeline.
- [ ] **Manager conversation** — One-on-one, use script, offer to share handover plan.
- [ ] **Send handover doc** — Within 24–48 hours of the conversation.
- [ ] **Confirm LWD in writing** — Email: “As discussed, my LWD is [date]. Please confirm.”
- [ ] **Update Atlas Copco** — Joining date and, if needed, buyout question.
- [ ] **Deliver on the plan** — Do the KT, update docs, be responsive until LWD. This is how you leave the door open and get a good release.

---

## What not to do

- **Don’t** go to HR first (unless your manager is unavailable or you have no choice). Manager first usually works better.
- **Don’t** badmouth the company or the role. Stay neutral and professional.
- **Don’t** promise to stay if they match, unless you genuinely would consider it.
- **Don’t** threaten (“I’ll leave anyway”) or say you’ll not serve notice. That’s where legal and reputation risk starts.
- **Don’t** disappear after they agree — deliver the handover. Your reputation and any future reference depend on it.
