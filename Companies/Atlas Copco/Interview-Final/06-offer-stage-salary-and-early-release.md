# Offer stage — Salary alignment + Early release

**Situation:** Atlas Copco is ready to release an offer once you submit documents. There has been no direct salary negotiation yet: you shared your range (20–22 LPA), they asked if it’s negotiable (“we can discuss, yes”), and they questioned the **steep raise from 13 to 22 LPA**.

This doc covers: (1) how to lock the number before submitting documents, (2) what to say when they push on “steep raise”, and (3) how to set up early release.

---

## 1. Order of operations — Don’t submit documents until number is clear

**Risk if you submit documents first:** They may release an offer at a lower band (e.g. 17–18 LPA) and put pressure: “We’ve already prepared the offer.” You’re then negotiating from a weaker position.

**Preferred sequence:**

1. **Get compensation clarity** (verbal or email) before you submit documents.
2. **Then** submit documents.
3. **Then** receive written offer; sign only after reviewing full CTC breakup.

**What to say when they ask for documents:**

- **Option A (direct):**  
  “I’m happy to submit the documents. Before I do, could we align on compensation? You’d mentioned the raise from my current CTC — I’d like to know what band you’re working with for this role so we don’t have a mismatch later. Once we’re aligned, I’ll send everything right away.”

- **Option B (softer):**  
  “I’ll get the documents to you by [date]. Could you also share the compensation band or expected CTC for this role when you send the document list? That way I can review everything together and confirm quickly.”

**If they insist “submit first, then we’ll share the offer”:**

- “I’d prefer to have at least a verbal alignment on the range before I submit — it helps me plan. What range do you have in mind for this position?”  
- If they still refuse: you can submit, but **as soon as** the offer letter arrives, if the number is below your floor (e.g. &lt; 20 LPA), reply with: “I’d shared my expectation of 20–22 LPA. The offer is at [X]. Can we discuss bridging the gap via fixed adjustment or a sign-on? I’d like to make this work.”

---

## 2. “Steep raise” pushback — What to say next

They’ve already said the 13 → 22 jump is steep. You’ve said “we can discuss, yes.” So the next time compensation comes up (call or email), you want to:

- **Not** over-justify or apologise.
- **State** once why the range makes sense, then **redirect** to their number or the full package.
- **Offer** a bridge (sign-on, early review) if there’s a gap.

### In a call / meeting

**When they say:** “We’re still looking at the number; the jump from 13 to 22 is quite steep.”

**You say (pick one, keep it short):**

- “I understand. I’d shared 20–22 as the range that works for me for this move — it’s in line with the role level and my experience. I’m flexible on how we get there — for example, if there’s a constraint on fixed, we could look at a sign-on or an early review. What range are you working with?”

- Or: “The 13 is from my current context; for this role and this move, 20–22 is what I’m looking at. I’m happy to see the full package — fixed, variable, benefits — and align. What can you share on your side?”

**Then stop.** Let them give a number or a range. Don’t fill the silence with more justification.

### In an email (if they’re coordinating over email)

You can send something like:

**Subject:** Re: Documents / Offer — Compensation alignment

Hi [Name],

Thank you for the update. I’m ready to submit the documents once we’re aligned on compensation.

You’d asked about the expectation of 20–22 LPA relative to my current CTC. That range is what I need to make this move — it’s in line with the role and my experience. I’m open to discussing how we get there (e.g. fixed vs sign-on or variable) if that helps on your side.

Could you share the compensation band or expected CTC for this role so I can confirm and send the documents promptly?

Best regards,  
[Your name]

| Item | Value |
|------|--------|
| Current CTC | 13 LPA |
| Your range | 20–22 LPA (fixed/base preferred) |
| Floor (below this, push for bridge or reconsider) | 20 LPA effective (fixed + sign-on or clear variable) |
| If they can’t do 22 fixed | Ask for sign-on to bridge; else variable with **written** payout terms |

**Bridge options (in order of preference):**

1. **Sign-on / joining bonus** — e.g. if they offer 19 fixed, ask for 2–3 L as one-time sign-on.
2. **Early salary review** — “Can we agree on a review in 6–9 months with a defined criteria to revisit the number?”
3. **Variable** — only if payout %, criteria, and date are in the offer letter.

## 4. Early release — How to set it up

You need to **request early release** from your current employer so you can join Atlas Copco without a long gap. Do this **after you have the offer in hand** (or at least a written/verbal commitment and expected joining date). Otherwise if the offer falls through, you’ve already signalled exit.

### Step 1: Know your contractual terms

- Notice period: **60 days** (from your prep).
- Check your offer letter / contract for: notice period, buyout clause, garden leave, non-compete.

### Step 2: Who to approach and in what order

1. **Manager first** (one-on-one, preferably in person or video).  
   Don’t go to HR first unless your relationship with your manager is very difficult.

2. **HR** — only if your manager says “this is an HR policy, I can’t approve” or if you need formal buyout/early exit paperwork.

### Step 3: What to say to your manager

Keep it professional and brief. You’re not obliged to name the company until you’re ready.

**Opening:**

- “I have an opportunity that I’d like to take up, and they need me to join by [target date, e.g. 4–6 weeks from now]. I’d like to request an early release so I can join without delaying them. I’m committed to a proper handover and documentation so the team isn’t left in a lurch.”

**If they ask “how much notice can you give?”:**

- “I can give [e.g. 30 days / 45 days] with a clear handover plan. I’m also open to discussing a buyout if that’s something the company allows.”

**If they say “policy is 60 days”:**

- “I understand the policy. I’m requesting an exception given my handover commitment and the importance of this move to me. Is there any flexibility, or would a buyout be an option?”

**Do not:**

- Badmouth the company or the role.
- Share Atlas Copco’s name until you’re comfortable (you can say “a product company in Pune” or “an MNC” if needed).
- Promise to stay if they match — only if you genuinely would consider it.

### Step 4: Handover plan (strengthens your request)

Prepare a short handover plan and offer to share it:

- List of owned features / modules / repos.
- Pending tasks and suggested owners.
- Documentation or runbooks you’ll update before leaving.
- Overlap period (e.g. 1–2 weeks with the new owner for knowledge transfer).

Offering this shows you’re not “disappearing” and makes early release easier to approve.

### Step 5: Atlas Copco — What to tell them

Once you’ve requested early release (or know the outcome), update Atlas Copco HR:

- **If early release is agreed:** “I’ve secured an early release. My last working day will be [date]; I can join from [date]. Please send the offer with this joining date.”
- **If still in discussion:** “I’m in discussion with my current employer for an early release. I’ll confirm the exact joining date by [date]. My contractual notice is 60 days; I’m pushing for [30/45] days.”
- **If they ask for written proof:** Some companies ask for the new offer letter or a letter from current employer. Have a clear last-working-day date (email from manager/HR) ready if needed.

Use or adapt the template in `notice-period-email-to-hr.md` when you write to Atlas Copco about notice period and joining date.

### Step 6: Notice-period buyout (optional)

- **What it is:** You (or the new employer) pay the current employer to release you before the end of the notice period. The amount is often 1–2 months’ salary or as per policy.
- **Atlas Copco:** When you’re on the offer call, you can ask: “If my current employer agrees to early release only with a buyout, does Atlas Copco support notice-period buyout?” Some companies reimburse or add it as a one-time payment.
- **Your current employer:** Check the contract or ask HR: “Is there a buyout option for notice period, and what would be the amount?”
